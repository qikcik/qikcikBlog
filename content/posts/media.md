# Media

(my short observations about the artifacts created by other people, please keep in mind that following text is full of spoilers in classical way of understanding this term.) 


## 30.03.2026: Fight Club (1999)

First of all, the narration, especially in the first half of the movie, is great. This is the kind of movie in which the main character is also the narrator, and the dynamic scene changes combined with inside commentary are amazing (similar to Forrest Gump).

Nevertheless, this movie has an odd storyline which, from my perspective, creates a deliberate dissonance. The fact that the physical body was able to act, burn down the house, and drive the car, while the narrator's consciousness was shut down and relegated to the role of a passenger, feels like a manipulated timeline. 
But the movie uses this visual lie to make potential interpretations broader and it adds more light to show the philosophical meanings underneath, so I decided to just ignore those narrative tricks.

I think that the thread about consumerism isn't the strongest plot of this movie. It is only one perspective on the problem space that the movie is trying to show. For me, it tells about how we are lost in all the abstract stuff that doesn't really matter after some point, 
and how much we want to do things that have real consequences, how much we deeply want to take responsibility.

Like the significant shift in the main character's mindset when he feels genuine remorse over the death of Bob. As the opposite of the fact that, at the beginning, the car product recall is calculated strictly based on how many humans need to die in order to mathematically justify the action.
For me this film also shows how much we want to have a mentor, how much we want to have friends that will help us improve. We want to have someone who will show and teach us how to act. We just want to find someone to help us navigate the world, because we feel like we are missing some manual about "how to live".

I don't want to interpret the ending in detail, but for me, it shows that the two sides of the main character's psyche were finally combined, joining the best from the two worlds.
The original personality at the end learned through friction and experience, without all the madness and hedonism of Tyler Durden, but without capitulating from the world around us. And for some reason, I think that he accepted his life situation and started to feel some form of hope.

For me, the strongest scene and the one that I want to remember from this film is the scene where they threaten the convenience store clerk with death in order to force him to reclaim his dreams and return to veterinary school. 
It shows in hyperbolic way what truly matters in life, and it shows that pressure isn't always that bad. Recalling the dialogue, they give him the best gift in his life.


## 25.03.2026: Dead Poets Society (1989)

To be honest, I do not understand why this movie is so iconic. 
The first half is a weird experience, filled with elements whose purpose I simply cannot understand. like when Keating speaks about independent thinking, almost all the students misinterpret it from a shallow, hedonistic perspective. For me also all of the narrative feels unnecessarily overwhelming.

Despite this jarring execution, there is a philosophical core. 
The strongest point is the demonstration that independent thinking is essential for a fulfilled life and building genuine confidence. 
It also effectively pinpoints the difference between good and bad authority, and how crucial the right mentorship is in creating identity.

However, the execution is fundamentally flawed by what I consider a cheap narrative trick. 
The suicide plotline directly contradicts all the philosophical messages the movie tried to convey earlier. 
The teacher explicitly gave the student a rational, time-delayed protocol: "wait until you are an adult, then execute your own path."

Instead, the movie attempts to romanticize an unnecessary destruction. 
It hides the deeper, more complex understanding of compromise and adapting to society while keeping and developing your own identity. 
It completely destroys any potential discussion about human communication and the art of negotiation.

The only student who truly learned something from Keating's lessons was the "new student" Todd. 
Unlike the rest of the class, who merely got  "inspired" he had the courage to honestly admit that he couldn't write the poem. 
He allowed the teacher to teach him through interaction and friction. He had doubts, but he gave the new method a try in a controlled environment. 
This allowed him to develop his actual identity, not just a unrealistic shallow copy of someone else.

In the end, he was the only one genuinely sorry about the tragedy, and I think he truly understood Keating's real intentions. 
When the student committed suicide, Keating’s facial expression showed that this was not what he intended. he was crushed that the boy didn't find enough courage inside to actually live his life while expressing himself.

There are much better movies that attack the exact same philosophical problem ("Carpe Diem") without these fatal flaws. 
Arrival demonstrates the concept of enjoying life even when we know it will end tragically. Meanwhile, Forrest Gump shows the pure essence of "Carpe Diem" in daily, practical execution.

## 24.03.2026: Forrest Gump (1994)

This is one of those movies that’s hard to put into words. And one of those I’ll definitely return to. The narrative execution is brilliant.
Narrative glue in the form of scenes on the bench, humorous consistent short snippets that almost break the 4th wall, the pacing, and the fact that nothing is shown by accident. it all just keeps you in constant tension. 
It’s like taking the best parts of watching TikToks, but without any of the flaws.

The film flawlessly exposes that pure rationalization and conventional logic—both intellectual and social—is just a narrowed perspective on a broader subject. 
Despite being perceived differently from the outside, the protagonist consistently follows a path of courage, loyalty, integrity, and honesty. He doesn't do this mindlessly. he does it intelligently and with a sense of hope that infects everyone around him.

What resonated with me deeply is how crucial an authority figure is, in the meaning of someone who shows you how to navigate the world and focus on what truly matters. (in the movie it was a mother of main character)
The movie effectively points out how important action and effort are. And how important it is to "be more" and authentically put your whole self into everything you do, even if others might see it as stupid.

For me, the film also perfectly demonstrates that great things are simple things, and sometimes simple solutions are the best.

## 22.03.2026: Arrival (2016)

The most interesting thing is how fast this movie flew by, despite the complete lack of action or emotional plot twists.
It's not a movie I will ever return to, but it has an interestingly calm narrative pacing, and a main thread that forces you to think in the background after watching it.
The film has a lot of logical inconsistencies like the lack of explanation on how a written language doesn't contain the concept of time, or expecting a translation of an unknown alien language based on a few seconds of audio at the very beginning.
(I consciously decided not to analyze every detail of the world-building. It seems that this is not the type of movie where the created world holds the actual value)

The strongest point for me was that somewhere halfway through, I actually got a bit lost in the story's continuity.
I started wondering if the "scenes" from the beginning were actually at the beginning, or if I dreamed them, or if my memory just got completely messed up.
This resonated heavily with the main thread: that language is the main factor in our perception of the whole world.

Paradoxically to all this, the movie left me with one final query: "if I knew exactly what my whole life would look like in detail, and that it would end tragically, would I still be able to enjoy the small moments?"
